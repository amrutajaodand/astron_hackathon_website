Introduction

We are entering a new era of astronomy with the massive radio telescope, called the Square Kilometre Array (SKA), coming online in the next five years. The SKA will generate around three Petabits (3 Ã— 1015) of data per second, which is more than 10 times the current global internet traffic.

Such an immense data rate presents unparalleled challenges to astronomical and information technology communities in terms of data storage, transport, exploration, and accessibility. We need to solve these data challenges so astronomers can easily convert all of that data into groundbreaking science on topics such as the Universe's evolution, very first galaxies and the search for extraterrestrial life.

The current radio astronomy era is also pushing the limits of our technology. A precursor to the SKA, the Low Frequency Array (LOFAR) telescope, produces 300 TB of data a month. Currently there is over 25 PB of data in distributed storage, which is comparable to the size of the storage required by data intensive companies such as Google.

This incredible data rate produced by LOFAR is already presenting interesting challenges in terms of the science platforms used by scientists, access to super computers with >1 petaflops of processing power, movement of data, and the development of different machine learning algorithms to investigate the data. Such high requirements can result in less scientific return from these new telescopes.

Our vision is to empower astronomers with a platform where they can move, reduce and rapidly sift through millions of celestial objects and find brilliant outliers without ever worrying about where and how the data are located. These are significant challenges whose solutions will impact the rest of the information technology community.

In this hackathon, we aspire to bring together expertise such as data engineering, data mining, cloud, machine learning and data visualisation etc. from both the astronomy and the coding community. Together we can find innovative solutions to interesting astronomical data issues using LOFAR data as a test bed. Will you accept the challenge?

Logisitcs

ASTRON's main headquarters are located in Lhee, near Dwingeloo, the Netherlands. Different routes to access ASTRON are detailed here.

There is a shuttle bus that runs from Hoogeveen railway station to ASTRON at 8:30am (for a cost of Eur 5). If you would rather take a taxi from Hoogeveen railway station to ASTRON, please send an email to secretaryastronomy@astron.nl. A taxi costs Eur 31,00 per ride, so it is advisable to share. Taxis are not always available at the station so it is suggested to pre-book a taxi before your travel with the secretaries of ASTRON.

Participants are requested to reserve their own accommodation at Fletcher hotel De Borken. ASTRON will be supplying all participants with a free night accommodation. Therefore, you will be requested to pay for your total number of nights minus one. Details on how to reserve your accommodation are below (do note that the reservation has to be done via email, not the website.)

How to reserve a room:
- send an email to info@hoteldeborken.nl
- mention 'ASTRON Hackathon'
- specify your full name, arrival and departure date, single or double use
- if you want to rent a bike, please mention that in your email (note that ASTRON is 1.8 km from the hotel. So it is advisable to hire a bike)

Price of the room:
Single use: Eur 67.50 per room per night including breakfast.
Double use: Eur 7.50 per person per night extra (so Eur 75.00, including breakfast for two).

The block reservation expires on 8 February 2018. After this date, rooms can still be reserved at the same price but only if available.
